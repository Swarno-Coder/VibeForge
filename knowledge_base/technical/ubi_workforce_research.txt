UNIVERSAL BASIC INCOME AND WORKFORCE PARTICIPATION — RESEARCH SYNTHESIS
=========================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
Universal Basic Income (UBI) remains one of the most debated economic policies
of the 2020s. This document synthesizes findings from major pilot programs
and academic studies through 2025.

STUDIES SHOWING DECREASED WORK MOTIVATION
-------------------------------------------

Study 1: Finland UBI Experiment (2017-2018)
  - Participants: 2,000 unemployed Finnish citizens
  - Amount: €560/month unconditionally for 2 years
  - Key finding: No significant increase in employment during trial period
  - Employment effect: +0.4 percentage points (not statistically significant)
  - Methodology: Randomized controlled trial by Kela (Social Insurance Institution)
  - Limitation: Only tested with already-unemployed population
  - Publication: "The Basic Income Experiment 2017-2018 in Finland" (2020)

Study 2: Seattle-Denver Income Maintenance Experiments (SIME/DIME, 1971-1982)
  - Participants: 4,800 low-income families
  - Amount: Guaranteed income floor
  - Key finding: Work hours decreased by 9% for husbands, 20% for wives
  - Methodology: Randomized experiment with negative income tax
  - Critics note: Cultural context of 1970s makes direct comparison difficult
  - Publication: US Department of Health and Human Services (1983)

Study 3: Iran Cash Transfer Program (2011-present)
  - Scale: Universal cash transfer to 72 million Iranians
  - Amount: ~$1.50/day/person (purchasing power adjusted)
  - Key finding: Labor force participation decreased by 2-3 percentage points
    among working-age adults. Effect concentrated in women and secondary earners.
  - Methodology: Quasi-experimental, difference-in-differences analysis
  - Publication: Salehi-Isfahani & Mostafavi-Dehzooei, World Development (2018)

STUDIES SHOWING INCREASED ENTREPRENEURIAL ACTIVITY
----------------------------------------------------

Study 1: GiveDirectly Kenya (2016-ongoing)
  - Participants: 20,000+ individuals across 245 villages
  - Amount: $22.50/month for 12 years (long-term arm)
  - Key finding: 30% increase in new business creation; 20% increase in
    asset ownership. No reduction in labor supply.
  - Methodology: Cluster-randomized controlled trial
  - Additional: Recipients invested in livestock, farm inputs, small enterprises
  - Publication: Banerjee et al., "Effects of a Universal Basic Income during 
    the pandemic" (2022, NBER Working Paper)

Study 2: Stockton SEED Program (2019-2021)
  - Participants: 125 randomly selected residents
  - Amount: $500/month for 24 months
  - Key finding: Full-time employment INCREASED from 28% to 40% over 2 years.
    Recipients used funds for stability that enabled job searching and entrepreneurship.
  - Methodology: Randomized controlled trial by University of Pennsylvania
  - Key detail: Many recipients started side businesses or freelance work
  - Publication: West & Castro, "Preliminary Analysis" (2021)

Study 3: Y Combinator / OpenResearch (2024-2025)
  - Participants: 3,000 individuals across Texas and Illinois
  - Amount: $1,000/month for 3 years
  - Key finding: Recipients reported 15% higher rates of entrepreneurial activity.
    Time spent on education/training increased 25%. Overall work hours decreased
    by 1.3-1.4 hours/week (modest effect). Quality of work improved — more
    participants moved from part-time/gig work to stable employment.
  - Methodology: Largest US RCT on unconditional cash transfers
  - Publication: OpenResearch preliminary results (2025)

METHODOLOGICAL ANALYSIS
-------------------------
- RCT studies (Finland, Stockton, Y Combinator) provide strongest evidence
- Quasi-experimental studies (Iran) are useful for scale but harder to isolate causation
- Duration matters: Short trials (<2 years) may not capture long-term behavioral shifts
- Amount matters: Small UBI supplements show different effects than subsistence-level transfers
- Population selection: Testing on unemployed vs. general population yields different results
- Sample sizes: Stockton (125) is too small for robust statistical conclusions
- Hawthorne effect: Knowledge of being observed may alter behavior

DATA-DRIVEN MIDDLE GROUND: HYBRID POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS
----------------------------------------------------------
1. Graduated UBI: Start with smaller amounts and increase if employment maintained
2. Conditional enhancement: Base UBI + bonus for job training or education
3. Regional variation: Urban areas may need different amounts than rural
4. Duration: Long-term commitment (5+ years) needed for meaningful behavioral change
5. Complementary policies: UBI works best alongside job training, childcare, healthcare
6. Fiscal source: Carbon tax-funded UBI aligns environmental and economic goals
